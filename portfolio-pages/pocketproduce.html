Pocket Produce

A study in prototyping.

Overview

This project was part of an HCDE course in prototyping methods. In this project, we used several prototyping techniques to evaluate the usability, feasibility, and desirability of our service design idea -- Pocket Produce.

  • Team: 4 Students
  • Timeline: 2 Weeks
  • Tools: Figma, Paper Prototype, Sewing, Laser Cutting, Video Prototype

Concept

The concept for our prototype was to create a way for users to easily identify if the food they are purchasing is local and in-season. Our product is an service where users can use an app to scan produce and see if it is in-season and/or local. Then, they can scan their receipt after they finish shopping in order to earn points based on purchasing local and/or in-season that allows them to recieve rewards. The rewards include fun pins that can be placed on the reusable bag we send them when signing up for the service!

Prototype Goals

We have five prototype artifacts lsited below. Each one has a specific goal to help us gain insight into our design and make future improvements on subsequent prototypes.

Paper: We chose this technique to create a low fidelity prototype of the app in order to understand what the flow and features of the app would look like. We were evaluating:

  • Usability: Does the flow make sense and is easy to use?
  • Desirability: Even at a simple glance, do users see themselves using a service like this?

    • Figma: We chose this technique in order to create a higher fidelity prototype of the app. We based the design of this prototype on the feedback we received from the paper prototype. We were evaluating:

  • Feasibility: Are all of the features we want to implement feasible in a mobile app?
  • Feasibility: Can the options we present to the user be communicated effectively with the app?

    • Laser Cutting/Pins:This prototype is a low fidelity way to get the look and feel of our rewards pins. We were evaluating:

  • Feasibility: Is the size of the pin appropriate for adding to the bag?
  • Desirability: Are pins appropriate for motivating users to use the app?

    • Sewing/Bag: This prototype is a sewed bag that users would recieve when signing up for our service. We were evaluating:

  • Feasibility: Can the bag carry a full supply of groceries? Can the lining be removable?
  • Usability: Are users able to take off and put on the lining?

    • Product Video: we chose this technique as we wanted a way for users to see how the whole system of receiving a bag, using the app while shopping, using the app after shopping, and receiving rewards(pins) would look like. We were evaluating:

  • Desirability: Is the process appealing to users?
  • Feasibility: Do users feel like this would work?

    • Implementation

    To implement our service, we used several prototyping techniques: paper, Figma, soft-goods, laser-cutting, and video prototyping. All of these methods help to communicate our service to others while receiving feedback and critique. Based on critiques of our prototypes, we have a better understanding of the limitations of our service, and how it would be received in the real-world.

    Paper Prototype

    The design of our app began with a paper prototype. The paper prototype serves as a way to quickly communicate features of the service, decide on the interaction flow, and visualize the hierarchy of information without spending a lot of time developing an actual app. While all prototypes are temporary tools to communicate ideas, the paper prototype was especially so, since after receiving feedback on it, we moved on to a more high-fidelity prototype.

    Figma Prototype

    Up next, we created a figma prototype based on user testing on our paper prototype. the Figma prototype allowed us to explore visual design and evaluate aesthetics as we were able to use color and iconography. Here is a link to the Figma.

    Soft Goods Prototype

    When looking to create the bag we decided that we wanted it to be reusable and offer something different then other reusable bags. We wanted it to be large enough to be able to carry all their groceries, have a removable lining in order to wash the inside when it got dirty, and a way to close the bag.

    Laser Cut Prototype

    To get the look and feel of our rewards system, we laser cut pins.

    Evaluation

    We conducted user interviews and usability testing in order to evaluate if our prototypes were successful and effective at addressing our design goals (listed above). Below are some questions we asked to evaluate our prototype with users:

    Paper Prototype
  • Did you have any confusion while onboarding?
  • Would you use a service like this? Why or why not?
    • Figma Prototype
  • After scanning produce, are you able to make an informed decision on whether to purchase the food item?
  • Is the visual system appealing to you?
    • Sewing Prototype
  • Can you remove the lining? How was the experience removing it?
  • Do you think this could carry the average amount of groceries you purchase?
    • Laser Cut Prototype
  • Are the pins something you would want to collect as a part of the bag?
  • How do you think the pins will affect your shopping habits?
    • Video Prototype
  • Do you understand the process of using Pocket Produce?
  • Is this process something that would be convenient or a burden to use in grocery stores?

    • Analysis

    After completing our testing with users, we had plenty of feedback. Our rewards system could be more clear. Users were confused about how to gain points, and how those translated into pins. Additionally, users wanted stronger material for the bag. Some users mentioned some incorporation of pricing into the app so users who shop on a budget can also participate. This is just some of the valuable feedback we received. Future work would build off this work. Overall, this project showed the incredible value of prototypes as communication tools. Even with little time and resources, we were able to receive rich feedback from users.
